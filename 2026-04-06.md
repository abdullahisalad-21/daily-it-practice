## 4.1 Scenario 1 — Network Funtime Company (Full Solution)
Network Funtime Company currently allows every employee to use a random laptop model purchased by HR. This creates major challenges for centralized management, troubleshooting, and long‑term maintenance. When devices fail, the company must deal with multiple vendors, different warranties, and inconsistent hardware support, which increases costs. By adopting a centralized identity system such as Active Directory or OpenLDAP, the company can standardize management and reduce complexity. This also enables consistent configuration, easier imaging, and better control over user accounts.

Implementing a strong password policy is also essential. With a centralized directory, the company can enforce minimum password length requirements and prevent engineers from creating weak or inconsistent passwords. This reduces security risks and eliminates the need to reimage machines when passwords are forgotten, since the sysadmin can reset credentials directly. It also reduces HR’s workload and improves overall security posture.

The company urgently needs an inventory tracking system. Without labels or records, auditing is impossible and devices are easily lost or duplicated. By labeling laptops, wiping data when employees leave, and storing devices for reuse, the company avoids unnecessary purchases and gains visibility into its assets. Inventory tracking also supports lifecycle planning, helping the company know when devices need replacement.

Vendor vetting is another important improvement. Standardizing on a single laptop brand improves supportability, reduces driver issues, and often provides bulk‑purchase discounts. Choosing a vendor with strong support history ensures faster repairs and better long‑term reliability.

Finally, OS setup and software installation should be centrally controlled. Allowing engineers to install any Linux distribution introduces inconsistency and security risks. Standardized OS images ensure everyone has the same tools, reduce troubleshooting time, and protect company operations.

A structured onboarding and offboarding process will further improve efficiency. Disabling accounts, backing up data, and reusing laptops prevents unauthorized access and reduces HR workload.

Overall, these improvements increase security, reduce costs, and create a more scalable IT environment.

## 4.2 Scenario 2 — W.D. Widgets (Full Solution)
W.D. Widgets – Process Review and Recommendations

W.D. Widgets relies heavily on a single file server and manual IT processes, which creates significant risks as the company grows. One of the most urgent needs is implementing a proper backup strategy. Customer data is critical to the business, yet there are no backups or versioning systems in place. Introducing automated full, differential, or incremental backups would protect the company from accidental deletions, corruption, or system failures. Automated backups also reduce the workload on the sysadmin and ensure consistent protection as the company scales.

Automation is another essential improvement. As the sole IT person, manually installing applications, mapping drives, and onboarding new users is not sustainable—especially with plans to hire hundreds of new employees. Automating user provisioning, software installation, and configuration tasks will improve consistency, reduce errors, and allow IT operations to scale with the company’s growth.

The company also needs an offsite or cloud‑based backup plan. Storing all data in a single physical location exposes the business to natural disasters, fires, or hardware failures. Offsite backups ensure business continuity and provide secure access to historical data. As the company grows, older data can be archived on cost‑efficient long‑term storage such as magnetic tape.

Customer data should not reside on a single server. This creates a single point of failure and increases the risk of permanent data loss. Distributing data across multiple servers or using redundancy mechanisms ensures that if one system fails, operations can continue without major disruption.

Finally, the company needs a structured disaster recovery plan. This includes preventative measures to reduce impact, detection systems to alert IT of potential issues, and recovery procedures focused on meeting acceptable RPO and RTO targets. Implementing RAID arrays and eliminating single points of failure will further protect critical customer data.

Overall, these improvements will strengthen data protection, reduce operational risk, and ensure the IT infrastructure can support the company’s rapid growth.

## 4.3 Scenario 3 — Dewgood (Full Solution)
Dewgood – Process Review and Recommendations

Dewgood currently operates with limited resources and a single IT administrator, which makes efficient processes essential. One of the first improvements needed is establishing a proper inventory system. At the moment, computers are purchased on the same day an employee is hired, which causes delays and inconsistent hardware. By tracking all company assets and reusing laptops from employees who leave—after securely wiping their data—the organization can reduce unnecessary purchases and avoid last‑minute hardware issues.

The company also relies on a single server that hosts multiple critical services, including email, file storage, and the website. This creates a major single point of failure. Separating these services across multiple servers or using virtual machines would improve reliability and ensure that one failure does not shut down the entire organization.

Onboarding should be automated to reduce the workload on the sole IT person. Automated user creation, authentication, authorization, and configuration ensure consistency and reduce security risks associated with manual setup. Similarly, offboarding must be improved. When employees leave, their accounts should be disabled, their data backed up, and their devices wiped and returned to inventory to prevent unauthorized access.

The current ticketing system is confusing, causing employees to bypass it and contact IT directly. Switching to a free, user‑friendly ticketing system and providing proper training would reduce IT workload and ensure issues are tracked and prioritized based on severity.

Backups are currently stored on a single disk that the IT administrator takes home daily, which is risky and inefficient. A cloud‑based backup solution would be more cost‑effective, secure, and scalable for a non‑profit organization. Additionally, a disaster recovery plan should be developed to ensure data restoration in case of system failure.

Finally, the company website frequently goes down because it shares resources with other services. Proper documentation, monitoring, and hosting the website on a dedicated server—or virtual machine—would improve stability. Implementing redundancy ensures the website remains available even during failures.
